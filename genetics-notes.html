🔬Key Cell Types
GametesReproductive cells (sperm and eggs)
Somatic cellsAll cells in the body except gametes and their precursors
Note: Small amounts of DNA also found in mitochondria and chloroplasts
⚡Asexual vs Sexual Reproduction
Asexual = identical offspring from one parent (e.g. amoeba via mitotic cell division). Occasional mutations can occur.
Sexual = two parents producing offspring with a unique genome.
🧬Sets of Chromosomes
  • →Ordered display of chromosomes = Karyotype
  • →Homologous chromosome = same gene at the same place on both strands
  • →X & Y = exception — sex chromosomes (XX = Female, XY = Male)
  • →Autosomes = all other chromosomes
  • →Diploid cell = any cell with two chromosome sets
  • →Haploid cell = single set of chromosomes
Each sexually reproducing species has a characteristic diploid and haploid number.
🔄Life Cycle
  • →Haploid sperm + Haploid egg fuse = Fertilisation
  • →Fertilised egg = zygote = diploid (two chromosome sets)
  • →Only cells NOT produced by mitosis = gametes — develop from germ cells in the gonads (ovaries + testes)
  • →Meiosis = gamete formation
⚙️Meiosis
Meiosis reduces the number of chromosome sets from diploid to haploid.
  • →Meiosis I → produces 4 daughter cells
  • →Meiosis II
Sister chromatidsTwo copies of one chromosome
AlleleDifferent versions of a gene
LocusThe precise location of a gene on a chromosome

3 events unique to meiosis (occur during Meiosis I):

  • →Synapsis and crossing over (prophase I)
  • →Alignment of homologous pairs at the metaphase plate (metaphase I)
  • →Separation of homologues (anaphase I)

Concept 14.1
🌿Mendel & the Laws of Inheritance
Mendel used the scientific approach to identify two laws of inheritance.
  • →Character = heritable feature that varies among individuals (e.g. flower colour)
  • →Trait = each variant for a character

Why peas? Mendel's advantages:

  • →Clear contrasting traits
  • →Short generation time
  • →Many offspring
  • →Controlled mating ability
Pea plants self-fertilise naturally. Mendel manually cross-pollinated by removing stamens and dusting pollen from another plant. Method = known percentages of offspring.
Mendel's detailed quantitative analysis led to: Law of Segregation + Law of Independent Assortment
🧪Mendel's Model — 4 Related Concepts
Developed to explain the 3:1 inheritance pattern observed consistently among F2 offspring.
1
Alternate versions of genes account for variations in inherited characters (alleles). Today can be related to chromosomes and DNA.
2
For each character, an organism inherits two alleles of a gene, one from each parent. Each somatic cell in a diploid organism has two sets of chromosomes (one from each parent).
A genetic locus is represented twice in a diploid cell, one on each homologue of a specific pair of chromosomes.
3
If the two alleles at a locus differ, the dominant allele determines the organism's appearance; the recessive allele has no noticeable effect on appearance.
4
Law of Segregation = the two alleles for a heritable character segregate during gamete formation and end up in different gametes. Sperm/egg only gets one of the two alleles. If identical alleles = present in all gametes.

🎲Law of Independent Assortment
Two or more genes assort independently — each pair of alleles segregates independently of any other pair during gamete formation.
Only applies to genes (allele pairs) located on different chromosomes (not homologous) or genes that are very far apart on the same chromosome.
Concept 14.2
🎯Probability Laws in Mendelian Inheritance
Like flipping a coin for which gene goes where.
Multiplication ruleMultiply the probability of one event by the probability of the other — determines probability of one event and the other occurring.
Addition ruleProbability that any one of two or more mutually exclusive events will occur = sum of individual probabilities. The multiplication rule gives individual probabilities which are then added.
Solving complex problems: Apply probability rules to crosses involving multiple characters — faster than a Punnett square. Larger sample size = closer results conform to predictions.
Concept 14.3
🔗Beyond Simple Mendelian Genetics
Inheritance patterns are often more complex than predicted by simple Mendelian genetics. Mendel's principles extend to diverse organisms and more complex patterns.

A single gene deviates from simple patterns when:

  • →Alleles are not completely dominant or recessive
  • →A particular gene has more than two alleles
  • →A single gene produces multiple phenotypes
⚖️Degrees of Dominance
Complete dominance = phenotypes of heterozygote and dominant homozygote are indistinguishable.
Incomplete dominance = phenotype of heterozygotes is intermediate between the two homozygous phenotypes.
Codominance = phenotypes of both alleles are exhibited in the heterozygote because both alleles affect the phenotype in separate, distinguishable ways.
The observed dominant/recessive relationship depends on the level at which we examine phenotype. Some dominant alleles are extremely rare — the recessive trait can be more common.
🩸Multiple Alleles & ABO Blood Groups
Most genes have more than two alleles. ABO blood groups are a classic example.
  • →3 possible alleles: IA, IB, i
  • →4 blood types: A, B, AB, O

🌐Pleiotropy & Epistasis
PleiotropyThe ability of a single gene to have multiple effects — responsible for many hereditary diseases.
EpistasisA type of gene interaction where the phenotypic expression of one gene alters that of another independently inherited gene.

Example: coat colour (BbEe × BbEe)
